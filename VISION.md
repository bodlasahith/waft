# Why Waft Exists

Meeting interesting people has never been easier.

Staying connected with them has.

At conferences, hackathons, meetups, career fairs, and social events, people exchange LinkedIns, Instagrams, phone numbers, business cards, or X handles—but the process is surprisingly fragmented.

Everyone prefers different platforms.

- Some people live on LinkedIn.
- Others primarily use Instagram.
- Developers often spend their time on X or GitHub.
- Some communicate through Discord or Telegram.

Even if two people have several platforms in common, finding the "right" one in the middle of a conversation is often awkward.

> "What's your Instagram?"

> "I don't really use Instagram."

> "LinkedIn?"

> "I never check it."

> "Do you have X?"

> "...I think so."

The conversation becomes about remembering usernames instead of continuing the connection.

Waft removes this friction.

Instead of asking **which platform** someone uses, you simply share your Waft.

One scan instantly provides every platform that person chooses to share, allowing both people to connect however feels most natural.

---

# The Problem

Today's networking tools solve identity.

They don't solve relationships.

LinkedIn stores connections.

Instagram stores followers.

Contacts store phone numbers.

Business cards store information.

None of them remember the context behind the relationship.

After enough conferences, most people eventually have hundreds of contacts they barely remember.

- Where did we meet?
- What event introduced us?
- Who connected us?
- What communities do we share?

The relationship itself disappears.

---

# Our Solution

Waft acts as a universal networking identity.

Instead of exchanging a single social platform, users exchange an entire digital identity.

Every scan creates a shared connection while preserving the context that made the relationship happen.

As users attend more events and meet more people, Waft gradually builds a living map of their professional network.

Rather than becoming another contact list, it becomes a visual history of the communities, events, and people shaping their career.

---

# Our Philosophy

## Relationships are more important than profiles.

Profiles describe people.

Relationships describe communities.

Waft focuses on the relationships between people rather than treating every user as an isolated profile.

---

## Context matters.

Knowing **who** someone is is useful.

Knowing **how you met** is even more valuable.

Every connection should preserve meaningful context whenever possible.

Examples include:

- Event
- Organization
- University
- Mutual connections
- Time
- Shared communities

---

## Your network isn't a list.

It's a graph.

Professional relationships naturally form interconnected communities.

Waft embraces this by representing users as a living social graph rather than rows inside a database.

Visualization isn't simply a feature.

It is the natural representation of how people connect.

---

## Beautiful interactions create memorable experiences.

Networking should not feel transactional.

Small details—motion, animation, polished design, premium QR cards, and delightful interactions—make exchanging identities feel personal instead of mechanical.

# Long-Term Vision

Waft is not trying to replace LinkedIn, Instagram, X, Discord, or other social platforms.

Instead, it connects them.

Those platforms describe pieces of a person's digital identity.

Waft becomes the connective layer between them.

As users continue attending events, growing communities, and meeting new people, their graph becomes richer.

Eventually, Waft aims to answer questions like:

- Who should I meet at this event?
- Which communities am I becoming part of?
- How has my network changed over the past year?
- Which relationships haven't I followed up with recently?
- Which people connect multiple parts of my network together?

Rather than storing contacts, Waft seeks to model relationships.
